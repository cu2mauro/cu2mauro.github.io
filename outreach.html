Beyond physics

Outreach, scouting, and life outside the office

Theoretical physics has a reputation for being arcane and disconnected from everyday life. I think most of it can actually be explained, at least in spirit, to anyone who is curious. This page collects my attempts to do so, plus the public-facing activities I am involved in.

What is it that I do?

In one sentence: I use ideas from string theory to help simulate what happens when two neutron stars collide, and to predict the gravitational-wave signal that future detectors might catch.

In a few more sentences: when two neutron stars merge, the matter at their core is compressed to densities far beyond anything we can reproduce on Earth. Nobody knows exactly how matter behaves in that regime — it might even undergo a sudden phase transition, analogous to water suddenly turning to vapor. If that happens, it leaves a fingerprint in the gravitational waves that the merger emits. My job is to model that fingerprint, so that when next-generation detectors come online we know what to look for.

Scouting

I am a group leader in the AGESCI scouting movement, the Italian Catholic scouting association. I deeply value the educational dimension of scouting and the way it teaches responsibility, curiosity and care for the natural world — values that are not so far from those of a healthy scientific practice. A good chunk of my weekends and summers belongs to this part of my life.

Other things I care about

Outside research and scouting, I enjoy hiking, board games, role-playing games (Dungeons & Dragons in particular — I play and I am slowly learning to be a Dungeon Master), and travel, with a clear preference for places that are off the beaten track and for the kind of food you only find by getting lost.

Meditation and the contemplative side of Christian spirituality are also important to me, and I try to give them their proper space in my weeks.
